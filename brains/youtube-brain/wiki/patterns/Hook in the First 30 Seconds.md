# Hook in the First 30 Seconds

Win the first 30 seconds or lose the video. YouTube treats roughly the first 30
seconds as
the intro and counts strong videos as keeping 50 percent or more of the audience
past that
mark, while field data shows 55 percent or more of viewers leave within the
first 60
seconds. The opening is the highest leverage edit you have, because no later
improvement
matters to a viewer who already left. This pattern is the practical core of
[[Audience Retention]].

## The pattern

1. Pay off the package immediately. The first sentence should confirm the viewer
   is in the right place, delivering on the [[Packaging]] promise.
2. Open a loop. Pose the question the video answers, or tease the payoff, so the
   viewer has a reason to stay.
3. Cut the throat clearing. No long intros, channel trailers, or "before we
   start" preambles in the first 30 seconds.
4. Show, do not promise. If you have a striking result, visual, or claim, lead
   with a glimpse of it.
5. Get to value fast. If a top moment occurs late, move some of it earlier, as
   YouTube's own guidance suggests.

## Why it works

The intro metric in [[Studio Analytics]] makes the cost of a weak open concrete:
the
percentage still watching after 30 seconds. A strong hook lifts that number,
which lifts
[[Watch Time and AVD]], which the [[Recommendation System]] reads as
satisfaction. The first
30 seconds set the slope of the entire retention curve.

## For Shorts

On [[Shorts]], the window is the first 1 to 3 seconds, the swipe versus watch
decision.
There is no room for a build up; the hook is the first frame and the first
sound. Design the
loop so the end flows back into the hook.

## Pitfalls

- A hook that overpromises is [[Misleading Clickbait]]; the loop you open must
  close honestly.
- Do not pad the rest of the video to recover; padding lowers satisfaction. See
  [[Chasing Views Over Satisfaction]].
