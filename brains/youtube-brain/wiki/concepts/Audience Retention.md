# Audience Retention

The audience retention graph plots the percentage of viewers still watching at
each moment
of a video. It is the single most diagnostic chart a creator has, because it
shows exactly
where attention is won and lost. YouTube Studio annotates it with four key
moments: Intro,
Top moments, Spikes, and Dips. Retention reports require a video at least 60
seconds long
with at least 100 views, and "typical retention" compares your latest videos of
similar
length so you benchmark against yourself, not strangers. Retention is closely
tied to
[[Watch Time and AVD]] and feeds the [[Recommendation System]] through
satisfaction.

## The four key moments

- Intro: the percentage of viewers still watching after the first 30 seconds.
  YouTube treats roughly the first 30 seconds as the intro and counts strong
  videos as keeping 50 percent or more past that mark.
- Top moments: segments where almost no one dropped off; study and repeat what
  worked.
- Spikes: moments rewatched or shared; often a payoff, a visual, or a quotable
  line.
- Dips: moments viewers skipped or left; these are your edit list for next time.

## The first 60 seconds decide most of it

Field data on more than 10,000 videos puts average retention near 23.7 percent,
with 55
percent or more of viewers lost within the first 60 seconds and only about 1 in
6 videos
clearing 50 percent retention. The opening is therefore the highest leverage
edit in the
whole video. See [[Hook in the First 30 Seconds]] for the concrete pattern.

## Absolute versus relative retention

- Absolute retention is the raw curve for your video.
- Relative retention (typical retention) compares your video to your own recent
  similar length uploads, which is the honest benchmark.
- The detailed activity report shows the absolute number of views per segment,
  so you can separate a small-sample wobble from a real cliff.

## Tactics that raise retention

- Open a loop: pose the question the video answers and delay the payoff.
- Use pattern interrupts: B roll, camera angle changes, on screen graphics, and
  pace changes reset attention.
- Front load value: if a top moment occurs late, move that content earlier, as
  YouTube's own guidance suggests.
- Cut dead air and resist filler, which lowers the satisfaction signal even when
  it raises raw minutes. See [[Chasing Views Over Satisfaction]].

## Shorts retention

For [[Shorts]], the decisive moment is the swipe versus watch decision in the
first 1 to 3
seconds, tracked by the "Viewed vs swiped away" metric. Loops reward a clean cut
from the
end back to the start. Target a high average percentage viewed, often 60 percent
or more.
