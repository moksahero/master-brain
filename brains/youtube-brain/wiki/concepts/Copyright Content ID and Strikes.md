# Copyright Content ID and Strikes

The single most important thing a creator must understand about copyright on
YouTube is that
a Content ID claim is not a copyright strike. Confusing the two causes needless
panic over
harmless claims and dangerous complacency about real strikes, which can end a
channel. This
is a distinct enforcement track from [[Community Guidelines Strikes]], and
getting it wrong
is one of the few mistakes that is hard to reverse.

## Content ID claims

A Content ID claim is auto-generated when an upload matches reference content.
YouTube
states claims are different from copyright removal requests and copyright
strikes and
usually do not impact the channel. The rightsholder can set a claim to block,
monetize, or
track the video, often geography-specific; a monetization claim commonly runs
ads on the
content and may share revenue with the uploader. A claimed-and-monetized long
video stays
viewable, though a 1-to-3-minute Short with an active claim is blocked, and none
of this is
a strike. See [[Revenue Streams]] for how claims affect monetization.

## Copyright strikes

A copyright strike results only from a valid legal copyright removal request.
Three
copyright strikes within 90 days subject the channel and associated channels to
termination,
with uploaded content removed and new-channel creation prohibited. A strike
expires 90 days
after it is applied, but only after the creator completes Copyright School, a
four-question
course. A removed live stream adds a 7-day streaming restriction, extending to
14 days for
further strikes.

## Fair use is not a shield

Fair use is a legal defense, not a guarantee, and YouTube cannot adjudicate it.
Disputing a
Content ID claim without a valid basis can prompt the rightsholder to file a
removal request
that, if valid, produces a strike. Dispute only when you have a genuine basis.
These
policies evolve; verify current wording before advising, per
[[Source Intake and Refresh]].

## How to apply

- Treat a Content ID claim as a monetization or visibility issue, not a strike;
  decide whether to leave it, edit, or dispute on the merits.
- Treat a copyright strike as a channel-ending risk; resolve it, complete
  Copyright School, and avoid a second.
- License or create your own music and footage to avoid claims entirely.
- Never assume fair use protects you from a strike.
