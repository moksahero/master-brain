# RPM and CPM

RPM and CPM are the two revenue metrics creators confuse most. CPM (cost per
mille) is what
advertisers pay per 1,000 ad impressions, before YouTube's cut. RPM (revenue per
mille) is
what the creator actually takes home per 1,000 video views, after YouTube's cut
and across
all revenue sources. RPM is the number that matters for a creator because it
reflects real
earnings; CPM is an advertiser side input. See [[Revenue Streams]] and
[[YouTube Partner Program]].

## Definitions

- CPM = advertiser cost per 1,000 ad impressions, pre split. Only ad supported
  views count.
- RPM = total revenue divided by total views, times 1,000. It blends ads,
  memberships, Super Chat and Super Thanks, Shopping, and YouTube Premium, after
  YouTube's share.
- RPM is almost always lower than CPM because not every view shows an ad and
  because RPM is post split.

## RPM by niche

RPM varies sharply by topic because advertiser demand varies:

- Finance and investing roughly 4 to 12 dollars.
- Business and marketing roughly 4 to 9 dollars.
- Technology roughly 4 to 10 dollars.
- Health and fitness roughly 2 to 5 dollars.
- Gaming roughly 2 to 4 dollars.
- Entertainment roughly 1 to 2 dollars.
- Shorts roughly 0.01 to 0.10 dollars (the band varies by source); see
  [[Shorts Monetization]].

## What drives RPM up

- Audience geography: US, UK, and similar markets command higher CPMs.
- Video length: 8 minutes or more unlocks [[Mid-Roll Ads]], adding ad slots.
- Niche: high intent commercial topics attract higher bids.
- Diversification: memberships and Shopping lift RPM beyond ads alone; see
  [[Revenue Streams]].
- Suitability: green rated content under the [[Advertiser-Friendly Guidelines]]
  earns full ad revenue.

## How to apply

- Read RPM in the Revenue tab of [[Studio Analytics]], and compare it across
  your own videos, not against strangers.
- If RPM is low, check geography, length, and suitability before blaming the
  niche.
- Lengthen high value videos past 8 minutes where the content earns it, never
  with filler.
- Verify every published RPM figure against a current source before quoting it;
  see [[Source Intake and Refresh]].
