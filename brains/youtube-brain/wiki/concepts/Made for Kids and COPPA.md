# Made for Kids and COPPA

The made-for-kids audience setting is a legal requirement, not a creative
choice, and
getting it wrong carries both YouTube enforcement and US Federal Trade
Commission liability.
It exists to comply with the Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA),
following
YouTube's settlement with the FTC and the New York Attorney General. Any creator
whose
content could appeal to children must understand it, because the setting
disables a large
set of features that growth and monetization depend on.

## The requirement

Creators are required to set existing and future videos as made for kids or not.
The
determination rests on factors such as subject matter, whether children are the
intended or
actual audience, child actors, characters, themes, toys, and games. The setting
can be
applied at the channel or video level.

## What gets disabled

On made-for-kids content YouTube disables a broad set of features: autoplay on
home, cards
and end screens (see [[End Screens and Cards]]), video watermarks, channel
memberships,
comments, the donate button, live chat and live chat donations, merchandise and
ticketing,
the notification bell, personalized advertising, miniplayer playback, Super Chat
and Super
Stickers, and save to playlist or watch later. The channel-level setting
additionally
removes Posts.

## The monetization impact

Critically, YouTube serves no personalized ads on made-for-kids content,
allowing only
contextual ads based on the video rather than the viewer, as required by COPPA.
This
typically lowers revenue and removes most fan-funding streams; see
[[Revenue Streams]] and
the targeting limits in [[Ad Targeting]]. Misclassifying audience can expose a
creator to
FTC liability and YouTube enforcement, which overlaps with
[[Community Guidelines Strikes]].

## How to apply

- Assess honestly whether your content is made for kids using YouTube's factors;
  do not guess to keep features on.
- Expect the feature and revenue limitations and plan the channel's monetization
  around them.
- Set the audience correctly at upload; the legal obligation is on the creator.
- Re-verify the rules periodically, as both YouTube policy and child-privacy law
  evolve; see [[Source Intake and Refresh]].
