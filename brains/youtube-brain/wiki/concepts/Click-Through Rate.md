# Click-Through Rate

Impressions click-through rate (CTR) measures how often viewers watched a video
after seeing
a registered impression on YouTube. It is the conversion rate of your
[[Packaging]], the
title and thumbnail unit, turning a shown thumbnail into a view. An impression
is registered
only when the thumbnail is shown for more than one second with at least 50
percent visible,
and it excludes external and embedded players, notifications, and background
tabs. CTR is
one half of a feedback loop with [[Watch Time and AVD]]: the click gets the
view, but
retention decides whether the video keeps traveling.

## The only official benchmark

YouTube states that half of all channels and videos have an impressions CTR
between 2 and 10
percent. That is the entire official benchmark, and averages mislead. CTR
depends heavily on
the [[Discovery Surfaces]] the video reaches:

- Search yields fewer impressions but higher CTR, often 8 to 15 percent, because
  intent is explicit.
- Browse and Suggested yield high impression volume but lower CTR, often 3 to 7
  percent, because placement is cold.

## Why a falling CTR can be success

YouTube's own worked example: a video at 9 percent CTR with 10,000 impressions
later sits at
3.5 percent CTR with 100,000 impressions. That decline is a sign of success,
because the
video earned 90,000 additional impressions by escaping its core audience into
broader
distribution. Judge CTR against impression volume and traffic source, never in
isolation,
and never right after upload when the core audience inflates it.

## CTR and retention are one loop

A high CTR with low [[Audience Retention]] is the clickbait trap: the click is
earned but
the promise is broken, so the system stops promoting the video and may apply
policy
consequences. See [[Misleading Clickbait]]. The native Test and Compare feature
reflects
this by choosing the winning [[Packaging]] on watch time share, not on raw
clicks. See
[[Title-Thumbnail A-B Test Loop]].

## How to apply

- Read CTR in [[Studio Analytics]] under Reach, split by traffic source, against
  your own 28 day average.
- If impressions are high but CTR is weak, the [[Thumbnails]] or [[Titles]] need
  work.
- If impressions themselves are weak, the topic or [[Packaging]] promise is not
  matching demand; that is a different fix.
- Improve CTR with [[Thumbnails]] and [[Titles]] discipline, then protect
  retention so the gains compound.
